Endurance training

Endurance training, also known as cardiovascular training, is any activity that elevates the heart rate and breathing rate.

Benefits of cardio training

Why you should do cardio exercises?

Cardio can improve the health and the functioning of your heart. It also can reduce the risk of heart disease.

Other benefits:

  • Healthier brain
  • Improves recovery from many diseases
  • Increases bone density
  • Expands lung capabilities and amount of air your lungs can hold
  • Helps to lower blood pressure and control blood sugar
  • Helps to burn calories and therefore lose weight
  • Reduces stress, boosts your mood and energy

Types of endurance workouts

What type of cardio should you choose?

Not all endurance workout is created equal, some types may work better than others for each person. There are 3 types of cardio training: high-intensity interval training (HIIT), moderate-intensity steady state (MISS) and low-intensity steady state(LISS)

Type of cardio Description
High-intensity It incorporates several rounds that alternate between several minutes of high intensity movements to significantly increase the heart rate to at least 80% of one's maximum heart rate, followed by short periods of lower intensity movements
Moderate-intensity Exercising at a consistent pace and intensity throughout the session. The goal is to get to a point where the exercise intensity is in a relatively stable heart rate and oxygen consumption.
Low-intensity Involves intensities of around 40 to 60% of maximum heart rate. It's something slow, easy, continuous and long (over 40 minutes)

Examples of exercises

Some of the best cardio exercises

The following exercises are some of the best endurance exercises that you can do without leaving your home.

Burpees:

Alternate between a plank position and jumping forward in the air. Make sure that your hands are flat on the ground and your back straight.

Squat jumps:

You start out in a squat, and then jump up, trying to get as high as possible, and then land back in the squat.

Mountain climbers:

Performed from plank position, you'll alternate bringing one knee to your chest, then back out again, speeding up each time until you're "running" against the floor.

Jumping jack:

Also known as a star jump, jumping jack is performed by jumping to a position with the legs spread wide and hands going overhead, and then returning to a position with feet together and the arms at the sides.

Inchworm:

You start standing, reach your arms down toward the ground, walk your hands away from your feet, entering a plank-like position, before stepping your feet forward toward your hands and finally returning to a standing position.
